Quote

Something that someone else said reproduced on your site.

Overview

Quotes are something someone else said. This separates them from Pull Quotes which are something you said, just fluffed up to look bigger. That difference being established, there appear to be 2 main ways of dealing with quotes:

  1. Short Quotes
  2. Long Quotes

Short quotes are quotes that you refer to inside the rest of your copy. They make up part of the paragraph, and don't interrupt it. They tend to be a sentence or less.

Long quotes are quotes that you break the flow of your content to show. Instead of just referring to it, you are basically asking the reader to consider it with context. They tend to be a paragraph or longer.

Candidates

  • Not specified.
  • <aside>
  • <div>
  • <p>
  • <blockquote>
  • <q>

Short Quotes

My instincts tell me that most people don't markup inline short quotes. A short little test of some well respected newspapers, bore this out. (I picked news as they are fastitdious about quoting. ) Most sites just use quotation marks. Probably due to a long historical lack of browser styling for this tag in Internet Explorer. It was added in Internet Explorer 8. So <q> wins this easily.

Long Quotes

The <blockquote> element has a long history of being misued presentationally to indent content. Because of that the actual purpose may have taken a backseat for awhile. For the most part, this is showdown between not marking it up at all, and using the semantic tag.

However the addition of the <aside> tag opens up the door for some confusion, as it is often used for pull quotes. Pull quotes and regular quotes sometimes get confused. So one might think to stick a long quote in an <aside>. Easy rule of thumb here, if you are highlighting something someone else said, use <blockquote>.

Verdict

Short Quotes

Use <q>.

Long Quotes

Use <blockquote>.
